# TADKB experiment: ontology generation with OntoNG, RAG and QASAR

## Overview

This experiment evaluates the behaviour of **OntoNG** on a real biomedical dataset related to the **three-dimensional organisation of the genome**, using **RAG** and **QASAR** as complementary components of the pipeline.

The data source used was **TADKB**, a resource focused on **topologically associating domains (TADs)**. TADs are genomic domains characterised by a higher frequency of chromatin contacts within the domain than with neighbouring regions, and are considered one of the best-studied structural units of 3D genome architecture. They are relevant for chromosome organisation and for the delimitation of regulatory contexts.

For this experiment, the following resource distributed by TADKB was used:

- `TAD_annotations.tar.gz`
- Source: `http://dna.cs.miami.edu/TADKB/download/TAD_annotations.tar.gz`

According to the original TADKB publication, this resource integrates TADs detected in multiple human cell types, together with complementary information on 3D structures, coding genes, lncRNAs and TAD families. It also includes TADs inferred with different calling methods, such as **Directionality Index (DI)**, **GMAP** and **Insulation Score (IS)**, and incorporates experimental variants such as **HiChIP** and **SPRITE**.

---

## Data preparation

Instead of selecting only a few files, this experiment used **all `.txt` files** contained in `TAD_annotations.tar.gz` in order to provide OntoNG with a broader and more heterogeneous representation of the domain.

A single tabular dataset, `tad_all_files.csv`, was generated by extracting and combining all `.txt` files from the archive. Each row of the resulting CSV represents one TAD observation and includes:

- `chromosome`
- `start_position`
- `end_position`
- `source_method`
- `cell_type`
- `calling_method`
- `resolution`
- `source_file`
- `database_name`

This transformation made it possible to adapt a dataset originally distributed as multiple flat files into a structured tabular format suitable for OntoNG.

---

## Pipeline configuration

A complete pipeline based on **OntoNG + RAG + QASAR** was executed.

### Main configuration

- **Generative model:** `gpt-5.4`
- **RAG backend:** BioGateway ontologies embedded in **Neo4j**
- **Number of ontologies loaded in RAG:** 15
- **RAG query settings:**
  - `enable_terms_lookup=True`
  - `enable_properties_lookup=True`
  - `enable_relations_lookup=True`
  - `top_k=15`

The objective was to test the highest possible degree of reuse of the available ontological context.

---

## Conceptual structure generated by OntoNG

The result obtained from the complete dataset was not a flat representation, but an ontology with a **relatively articulated conceptual structure**.

QASAR identified:

- **8 classes**
- a sufficient density of properties and relationships to consider that the ontology is not merely descriptive

Among the inferred classes were entities related both to the biological domain and to experimental/contextual metadata, such as:

- `TAD`
- `CellType`
- `CallingMethod`
- `Resolution`
- `SourceMethod`
- `SourceFile`
- `Database`
- an intermediate class for **TAD observation/annotation**

This is methodologically relevant because it shows that, after expanding the input to all files in the resource, OntoNG did not collapse everything into a single generic structure. Instead, it was able to separate domain entities from experimental/contextual entities.

The generated ontology also showed a simple and formally consistent hierarchy. QASAR reported:

- **Tangledness (structural complexity)** = `1.0`
- **Cohesion** = `5.0`
- **Consistency** = `5.0`

This suggests a small but coherent ontology, without major structural inconsistencies.

---

## RAG mapping results

In this execution, the RAG backend no longer returned an empty mapping. The output showed that the system correctly recognised the loaded ontologies and recovered **three explicitly mapped terms**:

- **TAD** → `http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/SO_0002304`  
  Score: `1.0`  
  Source ontology: `tad`

- **Database** → `http://purl.obolibrary.org/obo/NCIT_C15426`  
  Score: `1.0`  
  Source ontology: `crm2gene`

- **TADObservation** → `http://rdf.biogateway.eu/tad//tad_id`  
  Score: `0.5561`  
  Source ontology: `tad`

This is clearly better than previous executions, in which the system detected the availability of the ontologies but did not produce explicit reuse of vocabulary.

At this point, it can be stated that there is **partial reuse of reference ontological knowledge**, at least at the level of **classes/terms**. In particular:

- the alignment of `TAD` with `SO_0002304`
- the alignment of `Database` with `NCIT_C15426`

both with maximum score, indicate that the system was able to connect relevant concepts from the generated ontology with terms already present in the ontologies loaded in the backend.

### Important interpretation

The `mapped_relations` and `mapped_properties` blocks remained empty.

This result must be interpreted carefully. After consulting Andrea, who validated the technical side of the RAG module, it was confirmed that the parts related to:

- embedding loading
- storage
- retrieval

were working correctly in this case.

Therefore, the absence of explicit reuse of properties and relations should **not** be interpreted as a technical failure of the backend. Rather, it should be understood as a consequence of the behaviour of the model during graph generation from the input data.

In other words, factors such as:

- the structure of the dataset
- the way entities are represented
- the effective context received by the model

may condition whether reuse appears clearly in **classes**, but not necessarily in **properties** or **relations**.

This is especially important for the interpretation of the experiment: the result suggests that the effect of RAG on the final ontology depends on **how the model exploits the retrieved context**. In this case, semantic reuse was clear at the level of **terms/classes**, but not at the level of **properties** or **relations**.

---

## QASAR evaluation

The generated ontology was evaluated with QASAR, obtaining a **global score of 3.0978 / 5**.

This places the result in an **intermediate range**, meaning that the ontology can be considered structurally reasonable and usable, although still far from a highly enriched resource from the terminological and documentary point of view.

### Strong points

Several structural subcharacteristics were favourable:

- **Structural characteristic** = `3.43`
- **Cohesion** = `5.0`
- **Redundancy** = `5.0`
- **Formalisation** = `5.0`
- **Consistency** = `5.0`

This indicates that the ontology does not show major inconsistencies, nor a particularly disorganised structure, nor problematic redundancy.

In addition:

- **Depth of subsumption hierarchy** = `2.0`
- **Tangledness / multiple inheritance complexity** = `1.0`

This suggests a simple hierarchy without severe structural conflicts.

### Maintainability and functionality

The ontology also showed reasonable values in maintainability and functional adequacy:

- **Maintainability characteristic** = `3.47`
- **Functional adequacy** = `3.12`

Within this functional profile, the following values were especially notable:

- **Clustering capability** = `5.0`
- **Guidance capability** = `5.0`
- **Search/query consistency** = `4.2`
- **Schema and value reconciliation** = `3.67`

These results suggest that the ontology may be useful as a conceptual organisation structure and as a resource for coherent querying, even if it does not yet show a high degree of documentary maturity.

### Annotation and attribute richness

Other favourable metrics included:

- **Annotation richness** = `0.8889`
- **Attribute richness** = `1.8889`

Both reached their maximum scaled value. This suggests that the ontology has an acceptable level of annotations and class attributes/restrictions, reinforcing the idea that the result is not merely skeletal.

---

## Main limitations detected

Despite the positive aspects above, QASAR also revealed clear limitations.

### Lack of lexical enrichment

The most evident limitation was the absence of lexical enrichment:

- **Synonyms per class** = `0.0`
- **Synonyms per property** = `0.0`
- **Synonyms per object property** = `0.0`
- **Synonyms per data property** = `0.0`

Accordingly, the incidence distribution showed:

- **Classes without synonyms**: `8`
- **Object properties without synonyms**: `7`
- **Data properties without synonyms**: `10`
- **Annotation properties without synonyms**: `10`

These incidences do not necessarily imply a logical flaw in the ontology, but they do indicate a limitation in terms of ontological maturity. The absence of synonyms reduces lexical interoperability and limits the usefulness of the resource for annotation, semantic search and natural language processing tasks.

### Annotation-related issues

Another group of incidences affected standard annotation properties such as:

- `dc:title`
- `dc:creator`
- `dcterms:description`
- `owl:versionInfo`
- `rdfs:label`
- `vann` properties

QASAR marked these as lacking name, description or synonyms. This pattern should be interpreted with caution, since it does not necessarily reflect a conceptual defect of the generated ontology, but rather the fact that QASAR evaluates standard annotation resources strictly, whereas automatically generated ontologies are usually not highly enriched at that level.

### Operability and quality in use

Additional low values were observed in:

- **Operability characteristic** = `2.26`
- **Quality in use characteristic** = `1.89`

This is coherent with the nature of the generated resource: an ontology that is formally acceptable and structurally consistent, but still not especially friendly from the perspective of human reuse, explanation and terminological documentation.

---

## Overall interpretation

This experiment supports several relevant conclusions.

### 1. Technical feasibility

The complete **OntoNG + RAG + QASAR** pipeline can be executed successfully on a real and relatively large biomedical dataset derived from TADKB. The integration of all `.txt` files into a single CSV and the generation of an evaluable ontology demonstrate the technical feasibility of the approach on real 3D genome organisation data.

### 2. Positive impact of the updated RAG backend

The experiment also shows that updating the RAG backend and recalculating the embeddings had a real positive impact. In this execution, explicit mappings to reference ontologies were recovered, especially for:

- `TAD`
- `Database`
- `TADObservation`

This supports the idea that, at least in this version of the experiment, the use of RAG contributed to a certain degree of **semantic alignment** between the generated ontology and the previously loaded ontological vocabulary.

### 3. Improvement is real, but partial

The improvement is not complete. Although useful mappings were obtained at the level of classes, no properties or relations were reused explicitly.

Given that the technical infrastructure of the RAG module was validated as working correctly, this result should not be attributed to a backend malfunction. Rather, it must be interpreted as part of the actual research question: **under which conditions does the model reuse classes, properties or relations from ontological context, and under which conditions does it not?**

### Final conclusion

In this case, the use of RAG with BioGateway ontologies did not automatically transform the generated ontology into a fully aligned and mature resource. However, it did provide **concrete and measurable semantic reuse** in relevant components of the model.

This makes the experiment valuable not only because it shows that external ontology vocabulary can be reused, but also because it shows that such reuse is **selective** and depends on the interaction between:

- the type of input data
- the conceptual graph generated by the model
- and the way the model exploits the retrieved ontological context

## Notes

This experiment is relevant for the thesis because it provides a realistic case in which:

- RAG contributes to semantic reuse
- the reuse is detectable and interpretable
- but the effect is incomplete and not uniform across all ontology components

This makes it useful not only as a positive example, but also as evidence that the impact of ontological context in LLM-based ontology generation is conditional rather than automatic.
